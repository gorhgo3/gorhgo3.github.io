Reflecting on core-learnings

What are three new things you have learnt about yourself and your ego due to the core learning?

I've recognized the importance of shifting from a competitive mindset to finding inspiration in others' achievements rather than feeling inadequate.
I tend to internalize feedback from others, sometimes turning it into self-doubt. While I'm open to being inspired by others, I can struggle with poorly presented feedback, which is an area I need to work on.
I've realized that I am a visual person when communicating with others. I derive comfort from seeing the person I am conversing with for visual feedback, and has become more apparent during core learning.

What are the role of values, empathy, and self-awareness in learning and programming?

Values, empathy, and self-awareness are crucial in both learning and programming. They help me recognize mental fatigue and stress, fostering a healthier learning approach. Moreover, in programming, I've learned that everyone has a unique problem-solving approach, and even in poorly written code, there can be valuable lessons.
I remember watching a 'worst movies with genius scenes' countdown- and I remember that even in a bomb there can be sections of beautiful code- logic or presentation.

What has surprised you the most about the core learning?

I was surprised by the relevance of core learning to coding. While I initially thought it might be unrelated, I found valuable insights. Neuroplasticity and the fixed vs. growth mindset concept were particularly enlightening. Learning how individuals with a fixed mindset felt compelled to prove themselves, while those with a growth mindset embraced inspiration, left a lasting impression.

What were the most challenging aspects of the core learning?

The most challenging part was staying motivated in sections I didn't find relevant. Some parts felt disconnected from my coding journey, leading me to question whether coding practice would be more beneficial. The extensive core content during the first and second sprint made it challenging to stay engaged. Not all advice resonates with everyone, and some core concepts aligned better with my experiences than others.

Why do you think we, a programming school, are spending so much time focusing on core learning in a web development Bootcamp course?

I believe EDA places a strong emphasis on core learning because of the fast-paced and competitive nature of the tech industry. My understanding is that there will always be individuals with exceptional technical skills. EDA encourages us to recognize the unique strengths and skills we bring from our previous careers and lives. This approach helps us market ourselves effectively, not just based on technical skills, but also on our interpersonal abilities and our capacity to learn and adapt.
EDA is assisting us to understand that what makes us valuable to the industry is not necessarily going to be our technical skills after 3 months; rather, it will be our capacity to understand that our valuable skills have already been worked on over the course of our lives and careers to this point.

Does the time you spent studying core learning here feel like a waste of time? Should you have just used that time to practise programming instead? Justify your answer.

While some core sections felt excessive, I understand EDA's purpose in accommodating for diverse learners. Not everything resonates with everyone, but it ensures everyone has something to learn from.
Personally, I could have spent less time on certain core elements, but the valuable parts have stuck with me. Overall, I dont think it was a waste of time- I believe core concepts will assist me further down the road, and I'm aware that there will be a big learning process beyond EDA. Which this seems to be preparing us for when we encounter self doubt.
