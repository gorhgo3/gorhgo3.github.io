Blocked on a Simple Problem

What was the problem?

One problem I encountered was with the building security system. It failed to properly shut down at night, leaving a particular set of doors on a campus open. This issue was recurring, happening only at night, and the doors were fine during the day.

What problem-solving techniques did you use?

My approach to solving this problem was to apply problem-solving techniques I had learned throughout my career as an electrician. One technique that worked well for me was "halving the problem." I tried to split the problem in half and figure out if there was any voltage at the location where I was standing, or if the door wasn't getting any DC voltage to energize the mag lock.

How did you feel throughout the process?

I felt quite confused during this process. Although the problem was simple in nature, I didn't have access to the contacts needed to diagnose it properly. The equipment was delicate and well-sealed due to security concerns, making it challenging to troubleshoot. It took me quite some time before I actually stepped back and analyzed the full scope of the problem.

What did you learn?

In hindsight, I realized that this was a tricky problem with a simple solution. The key lesson was that I was so engrossed in the technical details that I failed to see the simplicity of the situation. What I needed was a second pair of eyes to help me see the solution. This experience taught me the importance of broadening my scope when problem-solving and seeking assistance when needed.

How was the problem ultimately resolved?

Ultimately the issue was resolved after finding a failed backup relay for after-hours building security. I had been so fixated on gaining access to the door that it took some time before I stepped back and analyzed the full scope of the problem. This analysis only occurred once I received assistance from a colleague who helped me locate the specific relays related to the issue with the particular door I was working on. This was so I could initially figure out where in the panel the relays for this particular door were located.

Elegantly Solved a Problem

What was the problem?

Another particular problem that comes to mind is a time when we were working in a pump room. Pump rooms are normally pretty hot, and as you walk into a pump room, you generally don't miss the seriously noticed heat of the room as a standout feature. However, this particular fault was occurring because of a failed pump, and usually with power pumps, we would look at the control panel and sensors related to those pumps in the system management.

What problem-solving techniques did you use?

In this particular case, as soon as we walked into the pump room and instead of feeling heat, we smelled burning plastic, particularly varnish. Varnish is associated with binding to the motor, and immediately, this is a telltale sign of the fault that doesn't need to be looked at in reference to sensors. This generally has something to do with the hardware of the actual physical appliance or unit. Without getting my testing equipment and panels, we were able to follow an alternative measure of following the fault trail and it led us to a burning or burned-out circulating pump in a hot water system.

How did you feel throughout the process?

Throughout this process, it taught me some valuable skills. I did feel like it was a relatively underwhelming solution as I do enjoy tinkering around and trying to find solutions to problems. In this particular case, I learned that I need to stop being too fixated on the technical side of a fault and sometimes look at the bigger picture. I also realized that recognizing other aspects beyond my technical abilities as an electrician were sometimes far more necessary. Just being present in a situation gives you a strong foundation for solving every problem, as long as you have a methodical approach.

What did you learn?

In this situation, I learned that sometimes, problem-solving involves more than just technical expertise. It's about being present in the moment, recognizing telltale signs, and taking a methodical approach to troubleshoot. Sometimes, the solution can be elegantly simple, and being open to different problem-solving techniques can lead to successful outcomes.

Problem-Solving Techniques

Pseudocode

I really like using pseudocode. It seems to me like it's a very easy way to put my thoughts in order and allow myself to look at the broader picture of things. It helps me apply a logical expression in my mind, allowing me to analyze the problem comprehensively and create a roadmap of what I need to produce. Pseudocode is not only a fault-finding technique but also a way to for me to clear my mind and focus exactly on the plan I've laid out for me.

Trying Something

I feel a little bit less confident with "trying something". When I focus on fixing an issue, especially in coding, where I may not know much, I find that I need to research or plan before taking action. When code changes constantly it stops me from having a clear idea exactly whats going on. For me trying something without a clear plan can lead to confusion and jumping around. Developing a clear problem-solving approach is essential for me.

Rubber Ducky Method

The rubber ducky method is perfect for me. I love it because my mind tends to wander quickly, and I get anxious. The rubber ducky method is great for my mental health, allowing me to offload concepts and thoughts onto paper. I don't use a physical rubber duck; instead, I talk into a microphone and articulate what I'm thinking into a text document. It's a manageable way to clear my mind, process my thoughts, and outline a step-by-step approach to solving problems.

Reading Error Messages

Reading error messages is generally manageable for me. However, when working with frameworks and libraries, fault diagnostics can be challenging. Errors sometimes appear deep within the codebase, making them hard to locate. Reading through several paragraphs to find the relevant message in the error response can be tedious. I prefer straightforward error messages to diagnose issues faster and with less clutter to look through.

Console.logging

I find console logging to be a versatile way to approach problems. It's particularly relevant to my previous job, where I could use it to check for feedback at specific points in a function or pipeline. However, I must admit that sometimes using console.log makes me feel a bit lazy, and it seems like I'm avoiding understanding exactly what my code is doing as I substitute understanding or thought with dumping a log at a point my code SHOULD be working.

Googling

I enjoy Googling and often refer to documentation like MDN docs or W3. Most of my learning and fault finding has been related to syntax or understanding, MDN and W3 a valuable resource for quick solutions.

Asking Your Peers for Help

I'm pretty good at asking for help, although I haven't had to do it much in the course so far. I believe in seeking assistance when needed, especially for more challenging tasks that may come later in the course. Asking peers for help is an essential part of the learning process, and I'm open to it.

Asking Coaches for Help

While I haven't worked with a coach in this course, I've found asking those responsible for me for help during my apprenticeship was never something I've avoided.

Improving Your Process with Reflection

Reflection is similar to the rubber ducky method but more focused. I spend time on reflections, examining my learning content, and looking for ways to improve. It's a chance to take an honest look at my progress, ask questions, and set a direction for my learning journey. Reflections provide me with valuable insights and help me steer my learning in the right direction. Often when there are excercises that I feel haven't benefitted as much at this stage in the curriculum. Reflections is my way of holding myself accountable for continuous learning.

Asking for Help and Learning from Past Experiences

In the past, I've had experiences of seeking help, particularly from coworkers or leaders who often preferred solve problems using workaround solutions if it meant saving time. Often, they would take the opportunity to demonstrate how simple a situation was or resolve it in a way that I didn't consider appropriate. As a result, I hesitated to ask for help, driven by a combination of genuine interest and practical considerations.

This was rooted in a desire to fully understand and master a concept before considering that a workaround was the only solution. While this approach aligned with my interest of growth, it sometimes clashed with time constraints and business considerations. It wasn't necessarily a matter of having a big ego; rather, I genuinely wanted to grasp concepts comprehensively.

Looking back, I realize that I enjoyed the process of fault-finding, and this inclination sometimes led me to prefer solving problems on my own. However, I also understand that there's value in seeking help and collaborating with others, as it can lead to more efficient and effective solutions.
